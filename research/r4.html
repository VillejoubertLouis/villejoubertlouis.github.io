1.Most CI fittings still use a default frequency-to-electrode table assigned at activation and rarely revisited — a one-size-fits-all decision encoded in the speech processor.
2.Why the mismatch is rarely measured
It needs imaging (CT/CBCT) for precise electrode localisation and a perceptual model to translate that into a listener-specific frequency map. Both are slow, costly, and not part of routine clinical pathways.
3.Why we built our own platform
Existing tools were either commercial black boxes or research-only Linux setups. Neither lets a clinician run a 5-minute mismatch screen at the chairside. So we wrote one — see the software section.

2.We used both fixed-list sentence tests and MUSHRA-style continuous scaling. The two diverged at moderate mismatch — speech held up, music fell.
3.Why mixed effects, not group means
Individual differences in mismatch tolerance are large enough that group-only statistics would smear the real story. Per-listener random effects keep the variance visible.
